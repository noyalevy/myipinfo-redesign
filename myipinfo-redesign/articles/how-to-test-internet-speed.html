How to Test Your Internet Speed: What the Numbers Mean

📅 February 2025⏱ 5 min read✍️ MyIPInfo Team

Download speed, upload speed, latency, jitter — your speed test results show several numbers. Here's exactly what each one means, what counts as a good result, and how to get the most accurate reading.

The Four Key Metrics

Every internet speed test measures four things: download speed, upload speed, latency (ping), and sometimes jitter. Each tells you something different about your connection quality.

Download Speed (Mbps)

Download speed measures how fast your connection can receive data from the internet. It's measured in Megabits per second (Mbps). This affects:

  • How fast web pages load
  • Streaming video quality (Netflix, YouTube)
  • Speed of downloading files and software updates
💡 Mbps vs MB/s

Speed tests show Mbps (Megabits per second). Your file manager shows download progress in MB/s (Megabytes per second). To convert: divide Mbps by 8. So 100 Mbps = ~12.5 MB/s actual file download speed.

Upload Speed (Mbps)

Upload speed measures how fast your connection can send data to the internet. Home internet plans are typically asymmetric — download speed is much higher than upload. Upload speed affects:

  • Video call quality (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet)
  • Live streaming (Twitch, YouTube Live)
  • Uploading files to cloud storage, sending large email attachments
  • Hosting a game server or website from home

Latency / Ping (ms)

Latency (also called ping) is the time it takes for a data packet to travel from your device to a server and back, measured in milliseconds (ms). Lower is better.

  • < 20ms — Excellent. Ideal for competitive gaming and real-time applications
  • 20–50ms — Good. Suitable for all online gaming and video calls
  • 50–100ms — Average. Fine for browsing and streaming, noticeable in gaming
  • 100–200ms — Poor. May cause delays in video calls and gaming
  • > 200ms — Very high. Real-time applications will feel sluggish

Jitter is the variation in latency over time. High jitter (even with low average ping) causes choppy video calls and lag spikes in games.

Tips for Accurate Speed Test Results

  • Use a wired connection — Ethernet gives more accurate results than Wi-Fi
  • Close other applications — Downloads, streaming, and updates consume bandwidth
  • Test multiple times — Run 3–5 tests and average the results
  • Test at different times — Network congestion varies by time of day (peak hours are typically evenings)
  • Test close to your router — If testing on Wi-Fi, proximity matters
  • Restart your router — Before testing, a router restart often improves speeds

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my actual speed lower than what I pay for? +
ISPs advertise "up to" speeds that you rarely achieve consistently. Factors include network congestion during peak hours, Wi-Fi signal quality, router age, distance from the ISP's infrastructure, and overhead from protocols. A wired connection typically gets closer to advertised speeds.
Does a VPN affect my speed? +
Yes. A VPN adds encryption overhead and routes traffic through an extra server, typically reducing speed by 10–30%. Modern VPN protocols like WireGuard minimize this impact. Test your speed with the VPN on and off to see its effect.
What is a good ping for gaming? +
Under 50ms is good for most online games. Under 20ms is excellent for competitive gaming. Above 100ms will cause noticeable lag, and above 150ms makes real-time competitive games very difficult to play well.
Why does my speed vary between tests? +
Network conditions change constantly. The test server load, internet congestion, your router's current state, and background processes all affect results. This is why averaging multiple tests gives a more reliable picture of your connection quality.
