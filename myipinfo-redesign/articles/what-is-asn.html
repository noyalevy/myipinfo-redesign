What Is an ASN? Autonomous System Numbers Explained

📅 December 2024⏱ 5 min read✍️ MyIPInfo Team

Behind every IP address is an ASN — the unique identifier of the network that owns it. ASNs are how the internet routes traffic between thousands of independent networks. Here's what they are and why they matter.

What Is an ASN?

An Autonomous System Number (ASN) is a unique numeric identifier assigned to a collection of IP networks and routers under the control of a single organization — such as an ISP, university, corporation, or government agency — that presents a unified routing policy to the internet.

Think of the internet as a network of networks. Each "network" is an Autonomous System (AS) — an independently operated collection of IP prefixes with its own routing policy. ASNs are how these systems identify themselves to each other.

💡 Real-World Examples

Google: AS15169 · Cloudflare: AS13335 · Amazon AWS: AS16509 · Comcast: AS7922

ASNs and BGP Routing

BGP (Border Gateway Protocol) is the routing protocol that glues the internet together. It uses ASNs to exchange routing information between autonomous systems. Here's how it works:

  1. Each AS announces which IP prefixes (ranges) it owns to its neighbors via BGP
  2. Routers build a map of reachable IP prefixes and the ASN paths to get there
  3. When a packet needs to travel from your ISP to Google, BGP determines the optimal path through the internet's AS topology
  4. The packet hops from AS to AS until it reaches AS15169 (Google) and the destination server

BGP runs between edge routers of different ASes — the "border" routers that connect one network to another. There are roughly 120,000+ active ASNs on the internet today.

Who Gets an ASN?

ASNs are assigned by Regional Internet Registries (RIRs):

  • ARIN — North America
  • RIPE NCC — Europe, Middle East, Central Asia
  • APNIC — Asia-Pacific
  • LACNIC — Latin America and the Caribbean
  • AFRINIC — Africa

Typically, organizations that need an ASN include: ISPs, large enterprises with multiple upstream providers, cloud providers, universities, CDN companies, and anyone who needs to announce their own IP space to the internet.

ASN Format

ASNs come in two formats:

  • 16-bit ASNs (original): 1–65535. Range 64512–65534 is reserved for private use.
  • 32-bit ASNs (extended, RFC 4893): Up to 4,294,967,295. Written as AS131072 or in dot notation as 2.0.

You'll typically see ASNs written as AS15169 or just 15169.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does my ASN tell someone about me? +
Your ASN identifies your ISP or network provider — not you personally. Someone seeing your ASN knows which company provides your internet service. This is public information registered with regional internet registries.
Why do VPNs have their own ASNs? +
Large VPN providers operate their own networks and own IP blocks, so they have ASNs like any other network operator. This means IP reputation databases can often detect known VPN ASNs. Some websites block traffic from known VPN ASNs.
What is the difference between ASN and IP address? +
An IP address identifies a specific device or interface. An ASN identifies the entire network that owns a range of IP addresses. One ASN can be associated with millions of IP addresses (e.g., Amazon AWS has billions of IPs under AS16509).
