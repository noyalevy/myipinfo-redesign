Public vs. Private IP Addresses: What's the Difference?

📅 February 2025⏱ 5 min read✍️ MyIPInfo Team

Your router has two IP addresses at the same time — a public one visible to the entire internet, and a private one used only inside your home network. Understanding the difference helps you troubleshoot connectivity issues, configure port forwarding, and protect your privacy.

Public IP Address

Your public IP address is assigned to your router by your Internet Service Provider (ISP). It is globally unique — no two devices on the internet share the same public IP at the same time. This is the address that:

  • Websites and servers see when you connect to them
  • Your ISP uses to route traffic to your home
  • Shows up when you visit MyIPInfo.io

Your entire household — every phone, laptop, TV, and smart device — shares one single public IP address. Your router receives all incoming traffic and sorts it to the right device internally.

Private IP Address

Your private IP address is assigned by your router to each device on your local network. These addresses are not visible on the internet — they exist only within your home or office network. Common private IPs look like:

  • Your laptop: 192.168.1.105
  • Your phone: 192.168.1.112
  • Your smart TV: 192.168.1.120
  • Your router itself: 192.168.1.1 (the gateway)
💡 Same Private IPs Everywhere

Millions of homes all use 192.168.1.x addresses — and that's fine! Private IPs are reused globally because they never appear on the internet. They're only meaningful within each individual local network.

How NAT Bridges the Two

NAT (Network Address Translation) is the technology that lets your router use one public IP to serve many private devices. Here's how it works:

  1. Your laptop (private IP 192.168.1.105) requests a webpage
  2. Your router receives the request and replaces the source address with your public IP
  3. The request goes to the website's server using your public IP
  4. The server responds to your public IP
  5. Your router remembers which device made the request and forwards the response to 192.168.1.105

NAT is why multiple devices can share one public IP — and also why unsolicited incoming connections from the internet can't directly reach devices inside your network (acting as a basic firewall).

How to Find Both IP Addresses

Find Your Private IP

  • Windows: Open Command Prompt → type ipconfig → look for "IPv4 Address"
  • Mac: System Settings → Network → select your connection → IP address shown
  • Linux: Run ip addr show or hostname -I
  • iPhone: Settings → Wi-Fi → tap your network → IP Address
  • Android: Settings → Network → Wi-Fi → tap your network → Advanced

Frequently Asked Questions

Can someone on the internet connect to my private IP? +
No. Private IP addresses are not routable on the internet — external parties can only see your public IP. To allow incoming connections to a specific device (e.g., a game server), you need to set up port forwarding on your router, which maps a public port to a private IP and port.
Why does my public IP change? +
Most ISPs assign dynamic public IPs that can change when you restart your router or after a lease period. Businesses that need a consistent address pay for static IPs. If you need a stable public IP for hosting, ask your ISP or use a Dynamic DNS (DDNS) service.
What is 127.0.0.1 (localhost)? +
127.0.0.1 is the loopback address — it always refers to your own device. When a developer runs a web server locally and visits "localhost" in their browser, the traffic never leaves the computer. It's not a private IP in the RFC 1918 sense, but a special reserved address.
Does a VPN change my private IP? +
No. A VPN changes your public IP (replacing it with the VPN server's IP). Your private IP assigned by your router stays the same. The VPN creates an additional virtual network interface with its own address, but your local network private IP is unaffected.
