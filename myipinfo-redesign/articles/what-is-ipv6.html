What Is IPv6? The Future of Internet Addresses Explained

📅 April 2025⏱ 6 min read✍️ MyIPInfo Team

The internet is running out of IPv4 addresses. IPv6 is the solution — offering 340 undecillion unique addresses. Here's everything you need to know about the protocol powering the next generation of the internet.

What Is IPv6?

IPv6 (Internet Protocol version 6) is the latest version of the Internet Protocol — the system that assigns a unique address to every device on the internet. It was developed by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) and formally standardized in 1998 to replace IPv4.

The core job of IPv6 is the same as IPv4: give every internet-connected device a unique address. But IPv6 does it at a dramatically larger scale, while also adding improvements to routing, security, and auto-configuration.

Why Was IPv6 Created?

IPv4, introduced in 1983, supports about 4.3 billion unique addresses. When the internet was designed, that seemed more than enough. But by the 2010s, with billions of smartphones, laptops, servers, smart TVs, and IoT devices all needing addresses, IPv4 space was exhausted.

The Internet Assigned Numbers Authority (IANA) allocated its last block of IPv4 addresses in February 2011. Regional registries have been managing the remaining pool carefully ever since — but the long-term fix is IPv6.

💡 Scale Comparison

IPv4: ~4.3 billion addresses. IPv6: 340,282,366,920,938,463,463,374,607,431,768,211,456 addresses — that's 340 undecillion. Every atom on Earth's surface could have its own IPv6 address with trillions to spare.

IPv6 Address Format

An IPv6 address is 128 bits long, written as eight groups of four hexadecimal digits separated by colons:

2001:0db8:85a3:0000:0000:8a2e:0370:7334

To make addresses shorter, IPv6 allows two simplifications:

  • Drop leading zeros in each group: 0db8 → db8
  • Replace one group of consecutive all-zero groups with ::

So the address above can be written as:

2001:db8:85a3::8a2e:370:7334

Special addresses include ::1 (loopback, equivalent to 127.0.0.1 in IPv4) and :: (unspecified address).

Key Differences from IPv4

  • Address space: IPv6 has 340 undecillion addresses vs. IPv4's 4.3 billion
  • Header format: IPv6 has a simpler, fixed-length header — faster to process
  • No NAT required: Every device can get a globally unique address, eliminating the need for Network Address Translation
  • Built-in security: IPv6 was designed with IPsec support from the start
  • Auto-configuration: Devices can configure their own IPv6 address without a DHCP server (SLAAC)
  • Better multicast: Replaces IPv4 broadcast with more efficient multicast

The IPv4 to IPv6 Transition

The internet hasn't switched to IPv6 overnight — it's a gradual transition that's been underway for years. Most networks today run dual-stack: they support both IPv4 and IPv6 simultaneously. When you connect to a website, your device and the server negotiate which protocol to use.

As of 2025, major ISPs and content providers like Google, Facebook, and Netflix fully support IPv6. Google reports that over 45% of its users access its services over IPv6.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is IPv6 faster than IPv4? +
In theory, IPv6 can be slightly faster because it has a simpler header and eliminates NAT overhead. In practice, the difference is minimal for most users. What matters more is your ISP and network quality.
Is IPv6 more secure than IPv4? +
IPv6 was designed with IPsec (a security protocol) as a mandatory component, though in practice it's often optional. IPv6 also eliminates certain attack vectors associated with NAT, but overall security depends more on proper configuration than the protocol version.
Will IPv4 be switched off? +
Not anytime soon. The transition is gradual. IPv4 and IPv6 coexist through dual-stack networking. A complete switchoff would require all devices and networks to support IPv6 — which will take many more years.
What does my IPv6 address look like? +
An IPv6 address looks like: 2001:db8:85a3::8a2e:370:7334. It's 128 bits written as eight groups of hexadecimal digits. You can check yours at MyIPInfo.io.
