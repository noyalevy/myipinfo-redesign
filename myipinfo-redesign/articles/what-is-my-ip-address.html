What Is My IP Address? A Complete Guide for Beginners

📅 May 2025 ⏱ 8 min read 👁 24,800 views ✍️ MyIPInfo Team

Your IP address is your device's unique identifier on the internet — like a home address, but for data packets. In this guide, you'll learn exactly what it is, how it works, how to find yours, and what it reveals about you.

What Is an IP Address?

An IP address (Internet Protocol address) is a unique numerical label assigned to every device connected to a computer network. Think of it like a postal address for your device — without it, data wouldn't know where to go.

Every time you visit a website, stream a video, or send an email, your device sends and receives data packets. Each packet contains the sender's IP address and the recipient's IP address, so the internet knows exactly where to deliver it.

💡 Quick Fact

IP stands for Internet Protocol — the set of rules governing how data is sent and received over the internet. It was first defined in 1974 and has been the backbone of internet communication ever since.

How IP Addresses Work

When you type a website address into your browser, a series of steps happen in milliseconds:

  1. Your device asks a DNS server to translate the domain name (e.g., google.com) into an IP address
  2. Your router assigns your request your public IP address
  3. The data packets travel across the internet to the destination server
  4. The server sends the response back to your IP address
  5. Your router directs the response to the correct device on your home network

This entire process happens so fast that you never notice it — typically under 100 milliseconds.

IPv4 vs. IPv6: What's the Difference?

There are two versions of IP addresses in use today:

IPv4 (Internet Protocol version 4)

IPv4 is the original IP format, introduced in 1983. It uses 32-bit addresses, written as four groups of numbers separated by dots:

199.203.79.17

IPv4 supports approximately 4.3 billion unique addresses. This sounds like a lot, but with billions of devices online, we've nearly run out. IPv4 address exhaustion is a real problem that IPv6 was designed to solve.

IPv6 (Internet Protocol version 6)

IPv6 uses 128-bit addresses, written as eight groups of hexadecimal values separated by colons:

2001:0db8:85a3:0000:0000:8a2e:0370:7334

IPv6 supports 340 undecillion addresses — that's 340 followed by 36 zeros. Every grain of sand on Earth could have billions of IP addresses. The transition to IPv6 is ongoing, with most modern devices and ISPs supporting both.

Public vs. Private IP Address

Your device actually uses two different IP addresses simultaneously:

Public IP Address

Your public IP address is what the wider internet sees. It's assigned to your router by your Internet Service Provider (ISP) and is unique globally. This is the IP address that websites, apps, and servers see when you connect to them.

Private IP Address

Your private IP address is what your router assigns to your device within your home or office network. These addresses are not visible on the internet and are reused across millions of networks. Common private IP ranges include:

  • 192.168.x.x — most home routers
  • 10.x.x.x — corporate networks
  • 172.16.x.x – 172.31.x.x — medium-sized networks
⚠️ Privacy Note

Your public IP address can be used to approximate your location (city level) and identify your ISP. Using a VPN replaces your real IP with the VPN server's IP, protecting your privacy.

How to Find Your IP Address

Find Your Public IP (Easiest Method)

Simply visit MyIPInfo.io — your public IP address is detected automatically and displayed at the top of the page, along with your location, ISP, and more details.

Find Your Private IP on Windows

  1. Press Windows + R, type cmd, press Enter
  2. Type ipconfig and press Enter
  3. Look for IPv4 Address under your network adapter

Find Your Private IP on Mac

  1. Open System Settings → Network
  2. Select your active connection (Wi-Fi or Ethernet)
  3. Your IP appears next to "IP address"

Find Your Private IP on Linux

ip addr show
# or
hostname -I

What Does Your IP Address Reveal?

Your IP address can reveal:

  • Approximate location — typically accurate to city level (not your street address)
  • Your ISP — the company providing your internet service
  • General region and timezone
  • Whether you're using a VPN or proxy — many IP reputation databases flag VPN exit nodes

What your IP address cannot reveal: your exact home address, your name, your browsing history, or your personal details. This information is only accessible through legal channels (subpoena to your ISP).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an IP address? +
An IP address (Internet Protocol address) is a unique numerical label assigned to every device on a network. It identifies the device and enables communication — like a home address for your computer on the internet.
Can someone find my location from my IP address? +
Yes, but only approximately. Your IP address can reveal your city, region, ISP, and country — but not your exact street address or personal identity. To get that information, someone would need a court order directing your ISP.
Does my IP address change? +
Most home internet users have a dynamic IP address that changes periodically (when you restart your router or after a set period). Businesses often pay for static IPs that never change. Your IP also changes when you use a VPN.
What is the difference between IPv4 and IPv6? +
IPv4 uses 32-bit addresses (like 192.168.1.1) and supports about 4.3 billion addresses. IPv6 uses 128-bit addresses (like 2001:db8::1) and supports 340 undecillion addresses. IPv6 was created to solve IPv4 address exhaustion.
How do I hide my IP address? +
The most effective methods are: (1) VPN — routes your traffic through another server, replacing your IP; (2) Tor Browser — routes traffic through multiple relays; (3) Proxy server — similar to a VPN but typically less secure.
